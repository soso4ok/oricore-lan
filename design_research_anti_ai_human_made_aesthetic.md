# Design Research: Anti-AI Human-Made Aesthetic

## Overview

The Anti-AI or human-made aesthetic is a reaction against the smooth, statistically averaged look of much generative design output, especially in branding, portfolio sites, and SaaS landing pages.[web:10][web:15] Its core appeal is not nostalgia alone; it signals authorship, taste, materiality, and selective restraint in a landscape crowded with generic AI visuals and templated UI patterns.[web:3][web:5]

For studio and portfolio websites, this usually means fewer claims, more confidence, more real work on screen, and less decorative interface noise.[web:15][web:24] The strongest examples do not merely look "handmade"; they feel edited, opinionated, and grounded in actual projects rather than AI-era credibility theater such as fake metrics, buzzword-heavy messaging, and interchangeable layout systems.[web:17][web:25]

## Studio Analysis

### basement.studio

The homepage uses a bold, full-width statement as its primary visual device rather than relying on dashboards, stat blocks, or conversion-style proof elements, which aligns with the current move toward confidence-led studio presentation.[web:15][web:17] The navigation is sparse, the copy is casual and human, and the project cards foreground recognizable work instead of vague claims about optimization or growth.[web:17][web:25]

What stands out most is the editorial pacing: large type, generous spacing, and a willingness to let the page breathe instead of filling every screen region with components.[web:17][web:22] That restraint is important because many AI-generated sites fail by over-explaining and over-populating the interface, whereas strong studio sites often communicate authority by omission.[web:24][web:25]

### quadroom.agency

This studio direction leans on a bright background, large imagery, and rotational presentation instead of a standard static hero with stacked copy and dual CTA buttons.[web:24][web:25] The emphasis stays on project visuals, which is a meaningful anti-slop move because it replaces generic promise language with evidence of actual taste and execution.[web:15][web:17]

The typography remains large and legible, and the navigation avoids the now-common AI pattern of a crowded top bar with badges, pills, and a highlighted CTA competing for attention.[web:24] The result feels less like a landing-page template and more like a designed portfolio environment.[web:17][web:22]

### rossery.com

This approach is portfolio-first: the page earns trust by showing work, named clients, and identifiable testimonials rather than trying to simulate legitimacy through arbitrary numbers.[web:15][web:25] That distinction matters because one recurring criticism of AI-generated web design is that it substitutes structure and proof with decorative confidence and unverifiable business metrics.[web:13][web:24]

Large section headings and readable scale choices also reinforce a human editorial sensibility.[web:17][web:22] Instead of tiny category labels and compressed information density, the site gives major content enough room to carry emotional and visual weight.[web:22][web:25]

## Shared Patterns Across Strong Human-Made Studio Sites

Across these examples, several recurring traits define the more credible, anti-slop direction:

- Real work is the main asset, not abstract claims about capability.[web:15][web:25]
- Typography does much of the branding work, often at large scale and with very little supporting chrome.[web:17][web:22]
- Navigation is minimal and does not try to force conversion instantly.[web:17][web:24]
- Social proof is concrete: client names, project titles, testimonials from identifiable people, and recognizable outputs.[web:15][web:25]
- Spacing is generous, which creates confidence and avoids the machine-made urge to fill every gap.[web:17][web:22]
- Visual rhythm is often asymmetric or editorial rather than perfectly centered and mechanically balanced.[web:10][web:24]

These choices read as human because they imply judgment. The page feels arranged by someone deciding what deserves emphasis, not by a model predicting what usually appears on a "modern agency website."[web:24][web:28]

## Common AI Website Tells to Avoid

The following patterns frequently signal AI-generated or AI-directed web design, especially in studio, SaaS, and "vibe-coded" landing pages.[web:23][web:24][web:27]

1. **Fake stats** — "47 clients," "100% satisfaction," "12M+ lines processed," or similarly specific but unverifiable numbers used as instant credibility props.[web:13][web:24]
2. **10px uppercase labels** — tiny overtracked eyebrow text that looks fashionable in isolation but weakens readability and has become a recognizable AI UI default.[web:24]
3. **Buzzword headlines** — phrases such as "Human-in-the-Loop," "Forward-Deployed Agents," or other inflated abstractions that sound strategic but communicate almost nothing.[web:24][web:25]
4. **Bento-grid metric cards** — repetitive 2x2 or 3x1 blocks of numbers and labels used as decorative structure rather than meaningful information.[web:23][web:27]
5. **Meaningless number clusters** — "100% / 0 / NDA / 48h" and similar stat mosaics that lack narrative context or user relevance.[web:24][web:27]
6. **The identical hero formula** — large headline, subtext paragraph, two buttons, background glow, then logos; this pattern is now so overused that it reads as generated by default.[web:23][web:24]
7. **Purple-to-blue gradients and heavy glassmorphism** — not inherently bad, but often used as instant "AI startup" camouflage without a real visual concept.[web:24]
8. **Generic copy** — language that could apply to any company, product, or studio because it was optimized for sounding modern rather than saying something specific.[web:24][web:25]
9. **Overly symmetrical layouts** — everything centered, evenly spaced, and perfectly balanced, removing tension, rhythm, and editorial point of view.[web:10][web:24]
10. **Default-font sameness** — relying entirely on safe defaults so the site has no typographic identity at all.[web:24][web:28]
11. **Decorative badges with no semantic role** — "Live," "New," or pulsing-dot UI markers that do not represent actual system state or new information.[web:24][web:27]
12. **Too many sections** — pages that keep adding blocks because the generator knows the pattern, not because the story requires them.[web:17][web:25]

## Why These Patterns Feel Fake

These patterns feel artificial because they are generated from statistical familiarity rather than design intent.[web:24][web:28] When a tool is prompted for a "modern studio site" or "clean SaaS landing page," it tends to reproduce the average of what already exists: similar gradients, similar heroes, similar cards, similar proof blocks.[web:24]

Professional designers usually work the opposite way. They start from a brief, context, audience, and narrative hierarchy, then decide what to show and what to remove.[web:17][web:19][web:25] AI slop emerges when teams ship the default pattern library without passing it through real editorial judgment, critique, and system-level refinement.[web:23][web:27]

## What to Do Instead: Human Design Patterns

The strongest alternative is not "make it messy" for its own sake, but to make visible that a person made selective, context-aware decisions.[web:10][web:15] In practice, the following patterns are more effective for a human-made studio site:

1. **Asymmetric layouts** — allow uneven weight, off-center emphasis, and editorial tension where appropriate.[web:10][web:24]
2. **Larger body text** — 16px minimum is a strong baseline, and 18–20px often supports a calmer, more premium reading rhythm.[web:22][web:25]
3. **Real social proof** — named clients, real project titles, credited testimonials, and links that can be verified.[web:15][web:25]
4. **Fewer sections** — compress the story to the essentials; confidence often appears as precision, not volume.[web:17][web:25]
5. **Typography as the hero** — let scale, weight, and phrasing do the heavy lifting instead of decorative UI tricks.[web:17][web:22]
6. **Whitespace as structure** — empty space should organize reading and emphasis, not feel like an area waiting to be filled.[web:17][web:22]
7. **One clear CTA** — many strong studios avoid hedging with equal-weight dual buttons because the page already knows what matters.[web:24][web:25]
8. **Specific copy** — say what the studio does, for whom, and with what kind of taste or method; avoid abstract universality.[web:24][web:25]
9. **Real imagery over symbolic filler** — project screenshots, photography, or artifacts from actual work outperform decorative AI placeholders.[web:15][web:24]
10. **A visible point of view** — the site should feel edited and opinionated, even if the styling is restrained.[web:3][web:15]

## Applied Changes for This Build

The current build direction already aligns well with anti-slop principles because it removes several of the most recognizable AI website tells and replaces them with a simpler, more editorial system.[web:24][web:27] The choices below form a coherent foundation:

- **White background** (`#FAFAFA`) for clarity, openness, and contrast discipline.
- **Near-black text** (`#111`) for strong readability and graphic sharpness.
- **Green accent** (`#2FCA54`) used sparingly so emphasis remains intentional rather than ambient.
- **No stats grids** because credibility should come from work, not invented numbers.[web:15][web:24]
- **No 10px labels** because readability and confidence matter more than trend-coded micro UI.[web:24]
- **No "Human-in-the-Loop" filler section** because vague strategic language weakens specificity.[web:24][web:25]
- **Larger animation / hero media** so the page leads with presence instead of explanatory clutter.
- **Larger text sizes throughout** to support an editorial, premium, human pace of reading.[web:22][web:25]
- **Fewer sections, more impact** so the narrative is curated rather than auto-expanded.[web:17][web:25]
- **Copy that sounds like a real studio** with opinion, confidence, and actual voice instead of startup boilerplate.[web:17][web:24]

## Missing Additions Worth Including

Several useful ideas were missing from the original draft and strengthen it as a practical design research note:

### 1. Typographic identity matters as much as layout

A site can avoid slop patterns and still feel generic if it uses purely default typography with no hierarchy or personality.[web:24][web:28] Font pairing, scale contrast, line length, and weight strategy are often what make a minimalist site feel authored rather than templated.[web:17][web:22]

### 2. Editing is a design skill

Human-made sites often feel better because they remove more than they add.[web:17][web:25] The anti-AI lesson is not only about texture or asymmetry; it is about curation, omission, and knowing when a section, badge, paragraph, or button does not need to exist.[web:15][web:24]

### 3. Social proof should be legible and attributable

Real trust signals are strongest when they are traceable: named clients, linked case studies, testimonials from identifiable people, and recognizable work outcomes.[web:15][web:25] Generic proof blocks without context increasingly read as AI-era theater rather than credibility.[web:13][web:24]

### 4. Anti-slop does not mean anti-technology

Professional designers still use AI for ideation, reference gathering, and production support, but they keep concept, hierarchy, and final judgment human-led.[web:31][web:19] The goal is not to reject tools; it is to avoid shipping the average output of those tools.[web:24][web:28]

### 5. Real content beats placeholder structure

Many weak AI-generated sites start with layout first and meaning second.[web:23][web:24] Better studio sites reverse that order: they use actual project material, real naming, and concrete statements, then shape the layout around the content.[web:15][web:25]

## Practical Design Rules

For future builds, these rules translate the research into usable direction:

- Show work before explaining process when possible.[web:15][web:25]
- Use one strong claim instead of three diluted claims.[web:17][web:25]
- If a metric cannot be explained, remove it.[web:13][web:24]
- If a label must be tiny to fit, the hierarchy is probably wrong.[web:22][web:24]
- If a section could belong to any startup, rewrite it until it could belong only to this studio.[web:24][web:25]
- If the page looks perfectly balanced everywhere, introduce some tension or variation.[web:10][web:24]
- If a visual effect has no communicative role, remove or reduce it.[web:24][web:27]
- If the design needs a second CTA to feel safe, the narrative probably lacks conviction.[web:24][web:25]

## Positioning Statement

A strong anti-AI human-made studio website should not look chaotic, nostalgic, or anti-digital by default.[web:10][web:15] It should look selective, authored, specific, and difficult to confuse with a generated template.[web:3][web:24]

The best direction is therefore not "make it look less polished" but "make it look more intentional." That means real work, real voice, strong type, edited structure, concrete proof, and enough asymmetry or restraint that the page feels designed rather than predicted.[web:17][web:24][web:25]
